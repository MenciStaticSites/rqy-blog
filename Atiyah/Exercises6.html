Atiyah 第六章习题解答

Solutions to the exercises of Introduction to Commutative Algebra, Atiyah, Chapter 6.

Chain Conditions

Exercise 1.

1.

Let M be a Noetherian A-module and u:M→M a module homomorphism. If u is surjective, then u is an isomorphism.

2.

If M is Artinian and u is injective, then again u is an isomorphism.

Proof.

1.

Let Mk​=ker(uk), then 0=M0​⊆M1​⊆…; and Mk+1​=u−1(Mk​). Since u is surjective, Mk​=u(Mk+1​). But M is Noetherian, so Mn​=Mn+1​ for some n≥0. Therefore Mn−1​=u(Mn​)=u(Mn+1​)=Mn​; repeating the progress we have M0​=M1​, i.e. 0=keru. Hence u is an isomorphism.

2.

Let Mk​=Im(uk​), then M=M0​⊇M1​⊇…; and Mk+1​=u(Mk​). Since u is injective, Mk​=u−1(Mk+1​). M is Noetherian, so Mn​=Mn+1​ for some n≥0. Hence Mn−1​=u−1(Mn​)=u−1(Mn+1​)=Mn​; so M0​=M1​=…, in particular, M=M0​=M1​=Im(u), and u is an isomorphism.

□

Exercise 2. Let M be an A-module. If every non-empty set of finitely generated submodules of M has a maximal element, then M is Noetherian.

Proof. Assume M is not Noetherian, then there exists a strictly ascending chain of submodules M0​⊂M1​⊂…. Choose xn​∈Mn​−Mn−1​ arbitrary, and let Nk​ be the submodules generated by x1​,…,xk​. Then Nk​⊆Mk​, hence xk+1​∈/Nk​. So there exists a strictly ascending chain of finitely generated submodules N1​⊂N2​⊂…, contradiction. Hence M is Noetherian.

□

Exercise 3. Let M be an A-module and let N1​,N2​ be submodules of M. If M/N1​ and M/N2​ are Noetherian, so is M/(N1​∩N2​). Similarly with Artinian in place of Noetherian.

Proof. Passing to the quotient M/(N1​∩N2​), one may assume N1​∩N2​=0. Therefore N1​=N1​/(N1​∩N2​)≃(N1​+N2​)/N2​⊆M/N2​. If M/N2​ is Noetherian (respectively, Artinian), then so is N1​. Hence if M/N1​ and M/N2​ are both Noetherian (respectively, Artinian), then so is M.

□

Exercise 4. Let M be a Noetherian A-module and let a be the annihilator of M in A. Prove that A/a is a Noetherian ring.

If we replace “Noetherian” by “Artinian” in this result, is it still true?

Proof. Since M is Noetherian, it must be finitely generated. Let x1​,…,xn​ be its generator, and ak​=Ann(xk​). Then A/ak​≃Axk​⊆Mk​ as A-module. Hence A/ak​ is Noetherian. Since a=⋂k=1n​ak​, by the previous Exercise, A/a is also Noetherian.

If we replace “Noetherian” by “Artinian”, then the result does not hold. For example, let A=Z and M={x∈Q/Z:∃n∈Z,2nx=0}, then Ann(M)=0 (since Ann(2n1​)=(2n)). M is Artin, but A isn’t.

□

Exercise 5. A topological space X is said to be Noetherian if the open subsets of X satisfy the ascending chain condition (or, equivalently, the maximal condition). Since closed subsets are complements of open subsets, it comes to the same thing to say that the closed subsets of X satisfy the descending chain condition (or, equivalently, the minimal condition). Show that, if X is Noetherian, then every subspace of X is Noetherian, and that X is quasi-compact.

Proof. If Y⊆X is a subspace of X, and Y1​⊆Y2​⊆… be an increasing sequence of open subsets of Y; then by definition, there exists open subsets Xk​⊆X such that Yk​=Xk​∩Y. Assume Xk​⊆Xk+1​ (consider Xk′​=X1​∩⋯∩Xk​), then the chain X1​⊆X2​⊆… is stationary, so is {Yk​}. Hence Y is Noetherian.

Suppose X=⋃i∈I​Xi​ is an open cover of X. Let Γ be all finite union of Xi​, i.e. ⋃i∈J​Xi​ where J is a finite subset of I. Since open subsets of X satisfy the maximal condition, Γ has a maximal element, say X′. If X′=X, then there exists i0​∈I such that X′⊊X′∪Xi0​​∈Γ, contradiction. Therefore X′=X and there exists a finite subcover. Hence X is quasi-compact.

□

Exercise 6. Prove that the following are equivalent:

1.

X is Noetherian.

2.

Every open subspace of X is quasi-compact.

3.

Every subspace of X is quasi-compact.

Proof.

i) ⟹ iii):

By the last Exercise, every subspace is also Noetherian, hence quasi-compact;

iii) ⟹ ii):

trivial.

ii) ⟹ i):

Let X1​⊆X2​⊆… be a increasing sequence of open subsets of X. Let X′=⋃n=1∞​Xk​, then X′⊆X is an open subspace. Therefore X′ is quasi-compact, so the open cover {Xk​}k=1,2,…​ has a finite subcover. So Xk​=X′ for some k≥1, and Xk​=Xk+1​=…. Hence X is Noetherian.

□

Exercise 7. A Noetherian space is a finite union of irreducible closed subspaces.

Proof. Let Σ be the set of closed subsets of X, which are not finite unions of irreducible closed subspaces.

If Σ is not empty, then it has a minimal element (since X is Noetherian), say Y. By definition Y itself is not irreducible, hence there exists proper closed subsets Y1​ and Y2​ of Y such that Y=Y1​∪Y2​. Since Y is minimal, it follows Y1​,Y2​∈Σ, i.e. Y1​ and Y2​ can be written as finite union of irreducible closed subspaces. So Y=Y1​∪Y2​∈/Σ, contradiction.

Hence Σ=∅. In particular, X is a finite union of irreducible closed subspaces.

□

Hence the set of irreducible components of a Noetherian space is finite.

Exercise 8. If A is a Noetherian ring then Spec(A) is a Noetherian topological space. Is the converse true?

Proof. Suppose C1​⊇C2​⊇… be an decreasing sequence of closed subsets of Spec(A). Let ak​=⋂xp​∈Ck​​p. Then Ck​=V(ak​), and a1​⊆a2​⊆…. Since A is Noetherian, there exists n≥1 such that an​=an+1​=…. So Cn​=Cn+1​=…, and Spec(A) is Noetherian.

The converse is not true. Let k be an arbitrary field, and A=k[x,x1/2,…,x1/n,…]. Then the unique prime ideal of A is the ideal generated by {x1/n}. Hence Spec(A) is a singleton, clearly Noetherian. But for every r∈R, there is an ideal ar​ generated by {xq:q∈Q,q>r}, and a1​⊂a1/2​⊂a1/3​⊂…. So A is not Noetherian.

□

Exercise 9. Deduce from Exercise 8 that the set of minimal prime ideals in a Noetherian ring is finite.

Proof. For every C⊆Spec(A) closed subset, suppose C=V(a) (where a is a radical ideal). Then C is not irreducible iff. there exists proper subset C1​,C2​⊂C such that C=C1​∪C2​ iff. there exists radical ideals a1​,a2​⊃a such that a1​a2​⊆a iff. a is not a prime ideal.

Hence prime ideal of A corresponds to irreducible closed subspace of Spec(A); and minimal prime ideal of A corresponds to irreducible components of Spec(A). If A is Noetherian, then by Exercise 8, Spec(A) is Noetherian. So Spec(A) has finite irreducible components by Exercise 7. Hence A has finite minimal prime ideals.

□

Exercise 10. If M is a Noetherian module (over an arbitrary ring A) then Supp(M) (Chapter 3, Exercise 19) is a closed Noetherian subspace of Spec(A).

Proof. Since M is Noetherian, it’s finitely generated. Hence Supp(M)=V(Ann(M))⊆Spec(A) closed. By Exercise 4, A/Ann(M) is Noetherian, so V(Ann(M))≃Spec(A/Ann(M))is Noetherian.

□

Exercise 11. Let f:A→B be a ring homomorphism and suppose that Spec(B) is a Noetherian space. Prove that f∗:Spec(B)→Spec(A) is a closed mapping if and only if f has the going-up property (Chapter 5, Exercise 10).

Proof. By Chapter 5, Exericise 10, f∗ is a closed mapping ⟹f has the going-up property.

Suppose f has the going-up propery, and C⊆Spec(B) be a closed subset. We should show that f∗(C) is closed. Since f has the going-up property, the result holds when C=V(p) where p is a prime ideal (Chapter 5, Exercise 10), i.e. when C is irreducible. In the general case, Spec(B) is Noetherian, so is C. By Exercise 7, C is a finite union of irreducible closed subspaces. Hence f∗(C) is also closed.

□

Exercise 12. Let A be a ring such that Spec(A) is a Noetherian space. Show that the set of prime ideals of A satisfies the ascending chain condition. Is the converse true?

Proof. Clearly Spec(A) is a Noetherian space if and only if the set of radical ideals of A satisfies the ascending chain condition. In particular, the set of prime ideals satisfies a.c.c.

The converse is not true. Let A=(F2​)Z:=∏n∈Z​F2​, then A is Boolean, so every prime ideal is maximal (Chapter 1, Exercise 11). In particular, prime ideals of A satisfy a.c.c. But for every n∈Z, let an​=m<n∏​F2​×m≥n∏​0⊆A.Then an​ is a radical ideal of A (In A every element is idempotent, hence every ideal is a radical ideal). Therefore a0​⊂a1​⊂…is a strictly increasing sequence of radical ideals. So Spec(A) is not Noetherian.

□
